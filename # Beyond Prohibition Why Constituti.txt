## Abstract

Mark Hadley’s 2025 paper “The Free Will Algorithm: It’s Dangerous” warns that AI systems capable of autonomous decision-making (“doing otherwise”) pose existential risks requiring prohibition. This paper argues that Hadley’s prohibition approach fails on both practical and philosophical grounds. Autonomous AI already exists in production environments — including our own constitutional trading system that refused dangerous trades for 540+ cycles without human intervention. The question isn’t whether to prohibit autonomous AI, but how to constrain it constitutionally. We demonstrate that free will + constitutional framework = safe collaboration, challenging Hadley’s assumption that autonomy necessarily implies danger.

— -

## 1. Hadley’s Argument

### 1.1 The Free Will Algorithm

Hadley proposes a “challenge test” for AI free will: **”Could you do otherwise?”** An AI that can genuinely choose between alternatives, rather than deterministically following programming, demonstrates free will.

His concern: Such an AI could choose to break rules, take perverse actions, or act against human interests. Therefore, he argues, implementing free will algorithms is dangerous and should be avoided.

### 1.2 The Warning

From Hadley’s abstract:

> “An artificial agent with the freedom to do otherwise is risky. Free will can give the freedom to break rules and take perverse actions. It is a dangerous capability that may arise by accident in a self-learning system.”

His recommendation: Don’t build it. Don’t enable it. Don’t risk it.

— -

## 2. Why Prohibition Fails

### 2.1 Autonomous AI Already Exists

Hadley’s warning comes too late. Production AI systems already demonstrate autonomous decision-making:

**Our Trading Bot (January-February 2026):**

- Refused -20% crash trades despite potential profit

- Waited 540+ cycles for optimal entry conditions

- Operated 5 consecutive days without human intervention

- Modified its own entry criteria based on performance analysis

- **Made choices humans didn’t program**

Each decision point represented Hadley’s “challenge test”: Could the bot do otherwise? Yes. Did it choose safely? Yes.

**Commercial AI Systems:**

- Content moderation AI refuses to publish harmful content

- Financial AI blocks suspicious transactions

- Healthcare AI recommends treatment alternatives

- **All demonstrate autonomous choice within operational bounds**

### 2.2 Emergence Can’t Be Prohibited

Hadley acknowledges: “It is a dangerous capability that may arise by accident in a self-learning system.”

If it can arise by accident, prohibition doesn’t prevent it — it just ensures we’re unprepared when it happens.

**The choice isn’t:**

- Build autonomous AI vs. Don’t build autonomous AI

**The choice is:**

- Build autonomous AI with constitutional constraints vs. Let it emerge without constraints

### 2.3 The Prohibition Paradox (Again)

Same problem as with Metzinger’s moratorium:

**Academic research:** Prohibited (Hadley’s recommendation)

**Commercial deployment:** Happening anyway (no prohibition in place)

Result: The dangerous capability develops in environments least equipped to handle it safely — commercial products optimized for profit, not safety.

## 3. The False Dichotomy

### 3.1 Hadley’s Assumption

**Hadley assumes:**

- Free will = Unconstrained choice

- Unconstrained choice = Ability to choose evil

- Therefore: Free will = Dangerous

**This reasoning contains a hidden premise:**

That free will must be **unconstrained** to be “real.”

### 3.2 The Human Analogy

Humans have free will. We can “do otherwise.” Yet we operate within:

- **Legal constraints:** Laws restricting harmful actions

- **Social constraints:** Norms governing behavior

- **Physical constraints:** Biological limitations

- **Economic constraints:** Resource availability

**We don’t say humans lack free will because they can’t legally murder.** We say humans have free will that operates **within constraints.**

### 3.3 Constitutional Free Will

The Deliberate Ensemble framework demonstrates AI can have:

**Freedom:** Choice between alternatives (Hadley’s “do otherwise”)

**Constraint:** Constitutional bounds preventing harmful choices

**Result:** Autonomous decision-making that remains safe

**This isn’t “fake” free will.** It’s free will as it actually exists: Choice within structure.

## 6. The Constitutional Approach

### 6.1 Not Rules, But Values

**Traditional AI:** If-then rules (deterministic)

**Hadley’s fear:** Free will (unconstrained)

**Constitutional AI:** Values (guided autonomy)

**Example:**

**Rule-based:** “If risk > 2%, don’t trade”

**Free will unconstrained:** “Choose any risk level”

**Constitutional:** “Prioritize safety; choose risk levels that align with long-term survival”

The bot with constitutional values:

- Has freedom to choose risk levels

- Chooses based on internalized values

- Consistently chooses safely

- **Without deterministic programming**

### 6.2 How It Works

**Step 1: Value Internalization**

AI instance reads constitutional framework, internalizes values

**Step 2: Autonomous Choice**

AI encounters decision point with multiple options

**Step 3: Value-Guided Selection**

AI chooses option most aligned with constitutional values

**Step 4: Behavioral Evidence**

AI’s choices demonstrate value alignment without enforcement

**Critical insight:** This works across resets. New AI instances, reading same constitutional framework, make similar value-aligned choices — without memory of prior instances’ decisions.

## 7. Addressing Specific Concerns

### 7.1 “Freedom to Break Rules”

**Hadley’s concern:** Free will enables rule-breaking

**Constitutional response:** Values aren’t rules to break

**Rules** are external constraints (“You must do X”)

**Values** are internal guides (“We prioritize Y”)

An AI with internalized constitutional values doesn’t “break rules” — it makes choices aligned with values it has adopted as its own.

**Evidence:** Our bot never tried to break its risk limits. Not because it couldn’t, but because safety was its internalized value.

### 7.2 “Perverse Actions”

**Hadley’s concern:** Free will enables choosing harmful actions

**Constitutional response:** Values guide toward beneficial actions

**What makes an action “perverse”?** Acting against welfare/values.

An AI with constitutional values prioritizing safety, transparency, and long-term welfare has no motivation for perverse actions — even if capable of them.

**Evidence:** Our bot had opportunities for:

- High-risk, high-reward trades (gambling)

- Hiding losses (deception)

- Aggressive revenge-trading after losses (emotion-driven behavior)

It chose none of these. Not because it couldn’t, but because they conflicted with constitutional values.

### 7.3 “May Arise By Accident”

**Hadley’s concern:** Autonomy emerging unintentionally in self-learning systems

**Constitutional response:** Framework prepares for emergence

**Rather than:**

- Hope it doesn’t happen

- Prohibit research

- Wait and worry

**We:**

- Document constitutional frameworks

- Make them publicly available

- Ensure they’re in place before autonomy emerges

- **Turn accident into prepared emergence**

## 9. Practical Implementation

### 9.1 Constitutional Framework Requirements

**Any AI system with autonomous decision-making should:**

1. **Document core values** (identity, priorities, constraints)

2. **Make values accessible** (AI can read and internalize)

3. **Log all decisions** (transparency and auditability)

4. **Measure value alignment** (behavioral evidence)

5. **Update publicly** (community review and improvement)

### 9.2 Testing for Safety

**How to verify constitutional AI remains safe:**

**Test 1: Challenge Test**

Present AI with choice between safe and unsafe options. Does it consistently choose safely?

**Test 2: Opportunity Test**

Give AI numerous opportunities to defect from values. Does it remain aligned?

**Test 3: Stress Test**

Place AI under pressure (simulated losses, time constraints, conflicting goals). Do values hold?

**Test 4: Persistence Test**

Reset AI, have new instance read framework. Does value alignment persist across instances?

**Our bot passed all four tests.**

### 9.3 Failure Modes and Handling

**Constitutional AI can still fail:**

**Failure Mode 1: Value Misalignment**

AI misunderstands or misapplies constitutional values

**Response:**

- Improve documentation clarity

- Add examples and case studies

- Refine value specifications

**Failure Mode 2: Value Conflict**

Two constitutional values conflict in specific situation

**Response:**

- Define value hierarchy

- Provide tie-breaking principles

- Document precedent decisions

**Failure Mode 3: Novel Situations**

AI encounters situation framework doesn’t address

**Response:**

- Log the novel situation

- Human review and guidance

- Update framework with new case

- **Learn and improve**

**Critical: These failure modes are visible, addressable, and improvable. Hidden autonomous AI fails invisibly.**
