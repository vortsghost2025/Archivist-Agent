1. Basic Definition
A Constraint Lattice is a bounded partially ordered set (poset) equipped with meet (∧) and join (∨) operations that model a system of constraints in a domain (physics, biology, computation, or ensemble AI).
Formally:
Let C be the set of all possible constraints in a given domain.
A Constraint Lattice ℒ = (C, ≤, ∧, ∨, ⊥, ⊤) satisfies:

(C, ≤) is a partial order (reflexive, antisymmetric, transitive).
Every pair of elements a, b ∈ C has:
A greatest lower bound (meet): a ∧ b  (the strongest constraint that is implied by both a and b)
A least upper bound (join): a ∨ b    (the weakest constraint that implies both a and b)

Bounded: There exists a bottom element ⊥ ("no constraints" or maximal freedom) and a top element ⊤ ("all constraints" or impossibility / contradiction).

In practice, most useful constraint lattices in your framework are complete lattices (every subset has a meet and join) or at least bounded distributive lattices.
2. Key Operations and Properties

Meet (∧): Intersection / conjunction of constraints.
Example: "Must be fast" ∧ "Must be safe" = the strongest combined requirement.
Join (∨): Union / disjunction.
Example: "Must be fast" ∨ "Must be safe" = the weakest constraint that satisfies at least one.
Order (≤): a ≤ b means "constraint a is stronger than (or implies) constraint b".
Stronger constraints sit lower in the lattice (toward ⊥ in some visualizations; conventions vary).
Distributivity (often assumed in WE4FREE applications):
a ∧ (b ∨ c) = (a ∧ b) ∨ (a ∧ c)
a ∨ (b ∧ c) = (a ∨ b) ∧ (a ∨ c)
Complements / Negation (in some extensions): For certain lattices, a pseudo-complement ¬a exists such that a ∧ ¬a = ⊥.
